Chapter 2: Acids and Bases - Notes

Introduction to Acids and Bases

Acids and bases are two categories of compounds that have opposite chemical properties. Acids release hydrogen ions (H+) when dissolved in water, while bases release hydroxide ions (OH-). The study of acids and bases is essential in understanding many chemical reactions, including neutralization and acid-base titrations.

Definition of Acids

An acid is a substance that donates a proton (H+) or accepts an electron pair in reactions. Acids can be classified based on their strength (strong or weak) and their ability to dissociate in water.

Examples of Acids:

  • Hydrochloric acid (HCl) - Found in stomach acid.
  • Sulfuric acid (H2SO4) - Used in batteries.
  • Acetic acid (CH3COOH) - Found in vinegar.

Definition of Bases

A base is a substance that accepts a proton (H+) or donates an electron pair in reactions. Bases can also be classified into strong and weak, depending on their ability to dissociate in water.

Examples of Bases:

  • Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) - Used in soap making.
  • Ammonia (NH3) - Found in cleaning products.
  • Calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)2) - Used in agriculture to neutralize acidic soils.

Acid-Base Theories

1. Arrhenius Theory

According to the Arrhenius theory, acids are substances that increase the concentration of hydrogen ions (H+) in water, and bases are substances that increase the concentration of hydroxide ions (OH-) in water.

2. Brønsted-Lowry Theory

In the Brønsted-Lowry theory, acids are proton (H+) donors, while bases are proton (H+) acceptors. This theory expands the concept of acids and bases beyond aqueous solutions to include non-aqueous solvents.

3. Lewis Theory

The Lewis theory defines acids as electron pair acceptors and bases as electron pair donors. This definition includes a wider range of compounds, such as metal ions.

Acid-Base Reactions

When an acid reacts with a base, a neutralization reaction occurs. This reaction forms water and a salt. The general equation for an acid-base reaction is:

Acid + Base → Salt + Water

Example of Neutralization:

When hydrochloric acid (HCl) reacts with sodium hydroxide (NaOH), the result is sodium chloride (NaCl) and water (H2O):

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H2O

The pH Scale

The pH scale is a measure of the acidity or basicity of a solution. It ranges from 0 to 14, where:

  • pH < 7: Acidic (e.g., lemon juice, stomach acid)
  • pH = 7: Neutral (e.g., pure water)
  • pH > 7: Basic (e.g., soap, bleach)

pH Formula:

pH = -log[H+]

Strong vs Weak Acids and Bases

Strong Acids

Strong acids completely dissociate in water, releasing all of their hydrogen ions. Examples include hydrochloric acid (HCl) and sulfuric acid (H2SO4).

Weak Acids

Weak acids only partially dissociate in water. Examples include acetic acid (CH3COOH) and citric acid (C6H8O7).

Strong Bases

Strong bases completely dissociate in water to release hydroxide ions. Examples include sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and potassium hydroxide (KOH).

Weak Bases

Weak bases only partially dissociate in water. Examples include ammonia (NH3) and magnesium hydroxide (Mg(OH)2).

Applications of Acids and Bases

  • Acids are used in the production of fertilizers, cleaning products, and in food preservation (e.g., citric acid in fruit juices).
  • Bases are used in soaps, detergents, and as antacids for treating heartburn.

Conclusion

Acids and bases are fundamental concepts in chemistry. Their interactions, such as neutralization, and their role in everyday life (e.g., in digestion, cleaning, and industry) are crucial to understanding both basic and advanced chemistry. Mastery of the properties, behaviors, and applications of acids and bases will help in numerous scientific fields.
