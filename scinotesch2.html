Notes - Chapter 2: Acids, Bases and Salts

Introduction

This chapter deals with acids, bases, and salts—substances that play a crucial role in chemistry and everyday life. You will learn their properties, uses, and the reactions they undergo.

Acids

  • Acids are substances that release H⁺ ions in aqueous solution.
  • They have a sour taste and turn blue litmus red.
  • Examples: Hydrochloric acid (HCl), Sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄), Acetic acid (CH₃COOH).

Bases

  • Bases release OH⁻ ions in aqueous solution.
  • They have a bitter taste and turn red litmus blue.
  • Examples: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH), Calcium hydroxide [Ca(OH)₂].

Indicators

  • Indicators are substances that change color in acidic or basic media.
  • Examples: Litmus, phenolphthalein, methyl orange.

Neutralization Reaction

A reaction between an acid and a base to form salt and water is called a neutralization reaction.
Example: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

Salts

  • Salts are formed when an acid reacts with a base.
  • They can be neutral, acidic, or basic in nature.
  • Examples: Sodium chloride (NaCl), Sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃), Potassium nitrate (KNO₃).

Important Compounds

  • Baking Soda (NaHCO₃): Used in baking, extinguishers, antacids.
  • Washing Soda (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O): Used in cleaning, glass, and paper industry.
  • Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄·½H₂O): Used in orthopedics and sculpture.

pH Scale

The pH scale ranges from 0 to 14 and measures the hydrogen ion concentration. A pH less than 7 is acidic, more than 7 is basic, and equal to 7 is neutral.
