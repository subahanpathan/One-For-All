Chapter 4: Carbon and its Compounds - Notes

Introduction

Carbon is a unique element due to its ability to form a large number of compounds. This chapter discusses the bonding in carbon, the concept of covalent bonds, versatile compounds of carbon like hydrocarbons, and functional groups.

Covalent Bonding

Carbon forms covalent bonds by sharing electrons. Covalent bonds can be single, double, or triple depending on the number of shared electrons. These bonds are strong and directional.

Versatile Nature of Carbon

Carbon shows tetravalency (forms 4 bonds) and catenation (ability to form long chains). This results in a vast number of carbon compounds with various structures:

  • Straight chains
  • Branched chains
  • Rings

Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons are compounds made up of hydrogen and carbon atoms. They are classified as:

  • Alkanes: Single bonds only (e.g., methane)
  • Alkenes: At least one double bond (e.g., ethene)
  • Alkynes: At least one triple bond (e.g., ethyne)

Functional Groups

Functional groups are atoms or groups of atoms that impart specific properties to the compounds. Common ones include:

  • -OH (alcohol)
  • -COOH (carboxylic acid)
  • -CHO (aldehyde)
  • -CO (ketone)

Soap and Detergents

Soaps are sodium or potassium salts of fatty acids. They work effectively in soft water. Detergents are effective in hard water too and are synthetic in nature.

Conclusion

Carbon's ability to form diverse compounds makes it the backbone of organic chemistry. Its compounds are fundamental in industries, biology, and daily life.
