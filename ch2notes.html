Notes - Chapter 2: Polynomials

Introduction to Polynomials

A polynomial is an expression consisting of variables (also called indeterminates) raised to non-negative integer powers, combined with coefficients. A polynomial can have one or more terms, where each term is a product of a constant (coefficient) and a variable raised to an exponent.

  • Monomial: A polynomial with just one term, such as 5x or 7.
  • Binomial: A polynomial with two terms, such as x + 1.
  • Trinomial: A polynomial with three terms, such as x² + 2x + 1.
  • Multinomial: A polynomial with more than three terms.

Key Terms in Polynomials

  • Degree of a Polynomial: The highest power of the variable in a polynomial. For example, in 4x³ + 3x² + 2x + 1, the degree is 3.
  • Leading Term: The term with the highest degree. For example, in 4x³ + 3x² + 2x + 1, the leading term is 4x³.
  • Constant Term: The term without any variable. For example, in 4x³ + 3x² + 2x + 1, the constant term is 1.

Operations with Polynomials

We can perform several operations with polynomials, such as:

  • Addition and Subtraction: Combine like terms (terms with the same degree).
  • Multiplication: Multiply each term in one polynomial by each term in another polynomial.
  • Division: Use long division or synthetic division to divide polynomials.

Examples

Consider the following polynomial:

3x² + 5x - 7

This polynomial has 3 terms. The degree is 2, the leading term is 3x², and the constant term is -7.

Important Theorems and Formulas

  • Factor Theorem: A polynomial f(x) has a factor (x - a) if and only if f(a) = 0.
  • Remainder Theorem: When a polynomial f(x) is divided by (x - a), the remainder is f(a).
  • Sum and Product of Roots: The sum and product of the roots of a quadratic polynomial ax² + bx + c are given by:
    • Sum of roots: -b/a
    • Product of roots: c/a

Applications of Polynomials

Polynomials have several real-world applications, including:

  • Modeling physical phenomena such as motion and growth patterns.
  • Solving real-world problems in engineering and physics.
  • Used in computer science for algorithms, especially in cryptography and optimization.
