Chapter 3: Metals and Non-metals - Notes

Introduction to Metals and Non-metals

Metals and non-metals are two distinct categories of elements based on their physical and chemical properties. Metals are typically good conductors of heat and electricity, while non-metals are poor conductors. This chapter explores the characteristics, differences, and uses of metals and non-metals.

Properties of Metals

Metals are elements that have the following properties:

  • Good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • They are malleable (can be hammered into thin sheets) and ductile (can be drawn into wires).
  • They are shiny and have a metallic luster.
  • Metals are solid at room temperature (except mercury).
  • They tend to lose electrons and form positive ions.

Properties of Non-metals

Non-metals have different properties compared to metals:

  • Poor conductors of heat and electricity.
  • They are brittle in solid form (if solid at room temperature) and not malleable or ductile.
  • They lack metallic luster and are dull in appearance.
  • Non-metals can be gases, liquids, or solids at room temperature.
  • They tend to gain electrons and form negative ions.

Differences Between Metals and Non-metals

Property Metals Non-metals
Electrical Conductivity Good conductors Poor conductors
State at Room Temperature Solid (except mercury) Gas, liquid, or solid
Malleability Malleable Brittle
Luster Shiny Dull
Ion Formation Lose electrons to form positive ions Gain electrons to form negative ions

Uses of Metals

Metals have a wide range of applications due to their physical properties:

  • Iron: Used in construction and manufacturing of machinery.
  • Aluminum: Used in aircraft, packaging, and electrical wires.
  • Gold: Used in jewelry, electronics, and as currency.

Uses of Non-metals

Non-metals also have several important applications:

  • Oxygen: Essential for respiration and combustion.
  • Carbon: Used in the form of graphite for pencils and diamonds for jewelry.
  • Chlorine: Used in disinfectants and plastics.

Metallic and Non-metallic Bonds

Metals tend to form metallic bonds, where electrons are shared freely among atoms, leading to properties like electrical conductivity and malleability. Non-metals, on the other hand, form covalent bonds where electrons are shared between atoms.

Conclusion

Understanding the differences between metals and non-metals is essential in chemistry. The physical and chemical properties of these elements dictate their behavior in reactions and their use in various industries. This knowledge is foundational to many branches of science and engineering.
