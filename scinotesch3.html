Notes - Chapter 3: Metals and Non-metals

Introduction

Metals and non-metals are two broad categories of elements with distinct physical and chemical properties. Understanding their differences and reactions is fundamental in chemistry.

Properties of Metals

  • Good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Malleable and ductile.
  • Usually solid at room temperature (except mercury).
  • Have a shiny appearance (lustrous).
  • Generally have high melting and boiling points.
  • Form positive ions by losing electrons (oxidation).

Properties of Non-metals

  • Poor conductors of heat and electricity (except graphite).
  • Brittle in solid form.
  • Can be solid, liquid, or gas at room temperature.
  • Usually dull in appearance.
  • Have low melting and boiling points compared to metals.
  • Form negative ions or share electrons (reduction or covalent bonding).

Reactivity Series of Metals

The reactivity series ranks metals from most reactive to least reactive, affecting how they react with water, acids, and oxygen. For example, potassium and sodium react vigorously with water, whereas gold and platinum do not.

Important Reactions

  • Reaction of metals with oxygen forming metal oxides.
  • Reaction of metals with water producing hydroxides and hydrogen gas.
  • Reaction of metals with acids producing salt and hydrogen gas.
  • Reaction of non-metals with oxygen forming acidic oxides.

Uses of Metals and Non-metals

  • Metals: Used in construction, electrical wiring, machinery, coins, jewelry.
  • Non-metals: Used in medicines, fertilizers, explosives, and as insulators.
