Notes - Chapter 4: The Age of Industrialisation

Introduction

This chapter discusses the historical process of industrialisation and how it shaped the world economy, particularly in Europe and India. It also addresses the myths about 'industrial progress' and explains how industrialisation was not uniform across the world.

Before the Industrial Revolution

Before industries, production was carried out in households and small workshops. The proto-industrial system in Europe involved merchants providing raw materials to peasants, who produced goods at home. This system laid the foundation for future factories.

The Industrial Revolution in Britain

Britain was the first country to industrialise due to its colonial wealth, coal and iron reserves, and capitalist investments. The textile industry, especially cotton, led the way. Innovations like the spinning jenny and steam engine boosted production.

Factories and Labour

Early factories had poor working conditions, long hours, and low wages. In India, workers often came from rural backgrounds and moved in and out of jobs frequently. Over time, industrial workers formed unions to fight for better conditions.

Handicrafts vs. Machine-made Goods

While machines made production faster, hand-made goods were still valued, especially for their quality and uniqueness. This created a market for both types of goods, especially in colonies like India.

Important Definitions

- Proto-industrialisation: A phase of industrial development before factories, where goods were produced by artisans at home.
- Industrialisation: The shift from manual labor to mechanised manufacturing.
- Spinning Jenny: A machine that allowed one worker to spin multiple spools of thread, invented by James Hargreaves.
